## Summary

Hosted by Daniel Murray of The Marketing Millennials, this episode features Amanda Natividad — VP of Marketing (Chief Evangelist) at SparkToro and co-creator, with Rand Fishkin, of the "Zero Click Content" / "Zero Click Marketing" framework, originally introduced in 2022 and later expanded into a book co-authored with Fishkin. The conversation traces how Natividad's framework evolved from an informal observation into one of the more widely cited content marketing concepts of the past several years.

The episode's core argument is that platforms — including LinkedIn — structurally reward content that delivers complete value without requiring a click to an external site, because platforms are economically incentivized to keep users on-platform. Natividad explains that this is not a reason to abandon content marketing but a reason to restructure it: instead of "teasing" value to force a click (the traditional content marketing playbook), marketers should deliver the actual insight directly inside the native post, which paradoxically builds more trust and is more likely to generate downstream engagement such as DMs, shares, and real conversations.

A significant portion of the discussion addresses measurement — specifically, how marketing teams should redefine what "good" content means when the traditional metric (click-through to a website) is deliberately suppressed by the platforms themselves. Natividad argues for reverse-engineering content around qualitative, conversation-generating outcomes (a reader saying "holy sh*t, that's me") rather than only optimizing for last-click attribution, and discusses how to align an internal team and leadership around these newer KPIs when stakeholders are used to click-based reporting.

The conversation also touches on Natividad's broader approach to developing "marketing taste" — the ability to judge what's genuinely good content versus what merely performs algorithmically in the short term — and how this taste is built through repeated practice and pattern recognition rather than formula-following.

## Key Ideas

- Zero Click Content delivers complete, standalone value natively on the platform (e.g., directly in a LinkedIn post) rather than teasing value to force an external click — because platforms structurally suppress reach for content with outbound links
- This is a deliberate adaptation to platform economics, not a retreat from content marketing — platforms want to keep users on-platform, so the most effective content meets that incentive rather than fighting it
- Marketing teams need new internal KPIs beyond click-through rate to evaluate zero-click content — qualitative signals like DMs, shares, and unprompted conversations are better indicators of impact than traffic
- "Marketing taste" — the ability to judge content quality independent of short-term algorithmic performance — is a skill built through repetition and is a meaningful differentiator between teams
- Giving away the "punchline" upfront in a post (rather than saving it to force a click) counterintuitively increases trust and downstream engagement, as demonstrated by Natividad's own promotional email test that became her highest-performing campaign of the year

## Why It Matters for B2B SaaS

Zero Click Content is one of the most directly applicable frameworks in this collection for B2B SaaS LinkedIn strategy, because it reconciles two findings that otherwise appear in tension across this research set: van der Blom's data shows that link-containing posts underperform link-free native posts, while SaaS marketing teams still need to drive demo requests, content downloads, and pipeline. Natividad's framework resolves this by reframing the goal — native, standalone-valuable content builds the trust and recognition that later converts via other channels (search, direct visits, sales conversations), rather than requiring every post to drive an immediate click.

For SaaS content teams, the practical takeaway is to treat LinkedIn posts as complete deliverables in themselves (a genuinely useful framework, insight, or data point), not as trailers for a gated asset — while still measuring success through engagement quality and downstream brand recognition rather than abandoning measurement altogether.
