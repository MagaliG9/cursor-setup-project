## Summary

This episode is a live panel discussion hosted by Dave Gerhardt, founder of Exit Five (the B2B marketing community), featuring Dasha Shakov (Head of Marketing, Proton.ai), Emeric Ernoult (Founder/CEO, Agorapulse), and Finn Thormeier (Founder, Project 33, an executive thought-leadership agency working with roughly 15 B2B SaaS companies). The panel breaks down what is actually working on LinkedIn heading into 2026, with an emphasis on practical execution rather than theory.

A central thesis of the discussion is that LinkedIn has evolved from a static "resume platform" into a genuine content and conversation platform, and that connection requests today function more like content opt-ins than passive networking. Gerhardt notes that when someone accepts a connection request, they are effectively opting in to see that person's future content — meaning the relevant question for B2B marketers isn't "how many connections do I have" but "how relevant is what I post to the people who've already opted in."

Ernoult, speaking as a 14-year bootstrapped SaaS founder who scaled Agorapulse to $25M+ in annual revenue with 160 employees, reframes the strategic question for company leadership: the "cheat code" to making social work for a B2B company is having a CEO who personally understands and participates in the channel, rather than delegating it entirely to a marketing team. Thormeier, whose agency specifically works with B2B SaaS founders and executives on LinkedIn content, adds the agency-side perspective on what separates effective executive thought leadership programs from generic ghostwriting.

The conversation also covers LinkedIn's Thought Leader Ads product, the perennial challenge of proving attribution for a channel where most value compounds indirectly (brand recognition that later lifts paid conversion, inbound, and sales conversations), and tactical advice for convincing skeptical leadership to invest meaningful time in the channel.

## Key Ideas

- LinkedIn connection requests function as content opt-ins — accepted connections have implicitly signaled interest in seeing that person's future posts, reframing post strategy around relevance to an already-opted-in audience
- The most reliable predictor of B2B social success is whether the company's CEO/founder personally understands and engages with the channel, not whether marketing has a "LinkedIn strategy" document
Organic LinkedIn presence improves the performance of paid LinkedIn efforts — audiences who recognize a brand or executive from the organic timeline convert better on paid media from the same account
- Attribution for LinkedIn content is inherently "messy" because most of its value (trust-building, brand recall, inbound from recognition) doesn't show up in last-click reporting — leaders need new mental models, not just better tracking
- Executive thought leadership agencies (like Project 33) work specifically with founder/exec accounts at B2B SaaS companies because that is where engagement and credibility concentrate, more than company pages

## Why It Matters for B2B SaaS

This episode is directly built around B2B SaaS practitioners (Agorapulse, Proton.ai, Project 33's client base), making it one of the more operationally specific resources on the list. The "connection requests are content opt-ins" reframe gives B2B SaaS marketers a more precise mental model for ICP-targeted connection campaigns tied to content strategy, rather than treating connection growth and content strategy as separate workstreams.

The recurring point about CEO buy-in being the actual bottleneck — more than tactics, templates, or content calendars — is a useful diagnostic for SaaS marketing leaders building the internal business case for founder-led content investment. It reframes the conversation from "what should we post" to "is our executive team willing to be the channel."
