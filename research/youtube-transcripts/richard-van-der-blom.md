## Summary

This video, produced by The Modern Banker's Public Library channel, walks through the headline findings of Richard van der Blom's 2025 Algorithm InSights Report — the sixth annual edition of his research, based on an analysis of over 1.8 million LinkedIn posts gathered from 58,000 individual profiles and 31,000 company pages across 60+ countries. While the publishing channel's primary audience is sales professionals, the content of the breakdown applies directly to organic content strategy generally, including for B2B SaaS marketing teams.

The video's central theme is that LinkedIn's organic distribution model fundamentally changed over the prior year: average reach and follower growth declined sharply across the board, while the platform simultaneously rewarded meaningful engagement (substantive comments, saves, and sustained dwell time) more heavily than in previous years. This is framed not as a platform decline but as a recalibration — LinkedIn shifted toward an interest-graph model that distributes content based on topical relevance and demonstrated audience interest rather than raw network size or posting frequency.

A significant portion of the breakdown addresses practical implications: which content formats are currently rewarded (document/carousel posts, polls with specific configurations, and short native video), how the first 30-60 minutes after publishing function as a critical algorithmic test window, and how company page content continues to be effectively invisible in the feed relative to personal profile content. The video also covers the shift in how external links are treated — once a near-universal reach penalty, now a more nuanced calculation depending on placement and post structure.

The breakdown closes by translating these findings into concrete recommendations for sales and marketing professionals: prioritize document-based educational content, treat the first hour after posting as an active engagement window rather than a passive one, and reduce reliance on company page posting in favor of individual contributor and executive content.

## Key Ideas

- LinkedIn's 2025 algorithm shift moved the platform from a network-size-driven distribution model to an interest-graph model, rewarding topical relevance and demonstrated audience interest over raw connections or follower count
- Document/carousel posts (PDF format) are currently the highest-engagement content type on the platform, followed by short native video; polls remain underused relative to their effectiveness
- The first 30-60 minutes after publishing function as an algorithmic test window — early engagement signals (especially substantive comments) determine whether a post receives further distribution
- Company page content continues to suffer from severe organic reach limitations compared to personal profile content, reinforcing the strategic case for founder-led and employee-led content over brand-account-led content
- External links are no longer an automatic reach killer, but they still carry a measurable penalty unless deployed carefully (placement, surrounding context, and post structure all matter)

## Why It Matters for B2B SaaS

Van der Blom's research is the most empirically grounded resource in this collection — built on a 1.8-million-post dataset rather than anecdote or single-company case studies — and this video translates that dataset into operational guidance. For B2B SaaS marketing teams specifically, the finding that company pages are "effectively invisible" in the feed provides hard data to support reallocating content effort toward founder and employee-led accounts, directly reinforcing the strategic direction also argued (without the underlying dataset) by Tommy Clark and Dave Gerhardt's panel guests elsewhere in this collection.

The document/carousel format finding is also directly actionable for SaaS product marketing: turning product education, customer outcomes, or framework content into PDF carousels is a low-cost, high-leverage format change supported by large-scale platform data rather than platform folklore.
