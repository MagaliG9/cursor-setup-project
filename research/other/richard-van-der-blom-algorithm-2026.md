# Summary

The Algorithm InSights Report 2026 is the seventh edition of Richard van der Blom's annual LinkedIn research, building on the 1.8-million-post analysis from 2025 with a new dataset reported at approximately 1.3 million posts. The headline finding is structural rather than incremental: LinkedIn has completed a shift from what third-party analysts summarizing the report describe as a "Relationship Graph" to an "Interest Graph" model of distribution. Under the old model, the feed was populated primarily by people a user was connected to. Under the 2026 model, only about 31% of the average feed comes from first-degree connections, roughly 25% comes from second- and third-degree connections, and around 10% is algorithmically "Suggested Posts" from accounts the user has never interacted with, surfaced purely on topical relevance. This means the foundational growth tactic of the last decade — build a large network, then post to it — is now substantially less effective than it used to be.

A central concept introduced or reinforced in this edition is "Topic Authority": an internal LinkedIn measure of how consistently and credibly a creator is associated with a specific subject area. The report finds that follower count and reach are now structurally decoupled — an account with 8,000 tightly-focused followers can outperform an account with 80,000 unfocused followers, because the algorithm is optimizing for topical relevance rather than raw audience size. Topic Authority is built from three inputs: topic consistency across posts, engagement quality, and "semantic clarity" — how cleanly a post's subject matter can be classified by the algorithm. Creators who post across unrelated subjects (a recurring example cited is a professional who alternates between regulation, leadership, and personal hobbies) effectively ask the algorithm to treat them as multiple different people, fragmenting and weakening their distribution.

The report also delivers updated, sometimes counterintuitive, tactical findings. Average reach has permanently reset to a lower baseline — the report is explicit that 2024-era reach levels are not returning — but average engagement is reported up roughly 18% over the prior six months of the research period, reinforcing the 2025 finding that the platform increasingly rewards depth over breadth. On posting frequency, the data shows two to four posts per week as the current sweet spot; moving from one post per week to two-to-four adds a meaningful average per-post impression gain, while daily posting produces a documented 26% drop in average reach per post and a longer-term "content fatigue" effect estimated at a 45% negative reach impact. On hashtags, the report finds they have been further demoted: posts without hashtags now outperform posts with hashtags by 5-10%, a reversal of long-standing platform advice, attributable to LinkedIn's removal of hashtag-following functionality and its shift toward reading full post copy and engagement history for topic classification instead.

On external links — one of the most consistently debated questions in LinkedIn strategy — the 2026 report finds that a single external link in a post body reduces median reach by 18.8%, and that the long-standing "put the link in the first comment" workaround no longer functions, since LinkedIn now suppresses comments containing external links with visibility reduced by as much as 80%. This finding sits in tension with a separate Q1 2026 analysis (by SayWhat, based on nearly 400,000 posts) which found that posts containing multiple external links, structured as curated resource lists, performed considerably better than posts with none — suggesting the relationship between links and reach is more about content quality and intent than a simple universal penalty. Third-party coverage of the report frames this as a genuine open question rather than a settled rule, recommending creators ask whether their specific goal is maximum reach (favoring no links) or qualified conversion (where links may be acceptable if they are integral to the content's value).

# Key Ideas

* LinkedIn has shifted from a Relationship Graph (connections-driven feed) to an Interest Graph (topic-relevance-driven feed) — only ~31% of the average feed now comes from first-degree connections, fundamentally changing what "growing your network" accomplishes
* "Topic Authority" — consistency, engagement quality, and semantic clarity around 2-3 closely related subjects — is now a stronger distribution lever than follower count; an 8,000-follower focused account can outperform an 80,000-follower unfocused one
* Reach has permanently reset to a lower baseline (2024 levels are gone), but engagement is up ~18%, reinforcing that the platform now rewards depth of interaction over raw audience size
* 2-4 posts per week is the optimal posting cadence; daily posting causes a documented 26% drop in average per-post reach and a longer-term content fatigue penalty (~45% reach impact)
* External links carry a measured reach penalty (single link: -18.8% median reach; link in comments: up to -80% visibility), but multi-link curated-resource posts can outperform link-free posts when the links are integral to the post's value rather than an afterthought

# Why It Matters for B2B SaaS

The Topic Authority framework is the single most actionable concept in this report for B2B SaaS marketing teams, because it directly resolves a common internal tension between "post variety" (covering company news, culture, product, and industry commentary) and algorithmic performance. For founder-led content and personal branding specifically, this report provides a data-backed argument for assigning each spokesperson (CEO, Head of Product, Head of Sales) a narrow, defined topic lane rather than letting them post broadly — directly reinforcing the kind of structured, topic-mapped employee advocacy program described in Pierre Herubel's team-led content framework. A SaaS founder who posts consistently about one or two themes (e.g., "category creation" and "founder lessons") will out-distribute one who posts inconsistently across product updates, hiring announcements, and general business commentary.

For demand generation and zero-click strategy, the updated link-penalty data gives B2B SaaS teams a more nuanced operating rule than "never link out": if the goal of a specific post is pure organic reach and brand-building, native content with no links will outperform; if the goal is to deliver a genuinely valuable curated resource (e.g., "10 tools we use to run demand gen," with links to each), the data suggests this format can succeed despite containing multiple links, because engagement quality appears to override the platform's link-suppression mechanism. This nuance allows SaaS marketing teams to deploy a hybrid content calendar — native, link-free posts for reach and brand-building; intentional, value-dense linked posts for direct lead generation and community resource-sharing — rather than treating "no links, ever" as an absolute rule.
