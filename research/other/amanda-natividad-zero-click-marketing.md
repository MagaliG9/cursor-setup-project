# Summary

Zero Click Marketing (ZCM) is a content and brand-building framework co-created by Amanda Natividad (Chief Evangelist at SparkToro) and Rand Fishkin, first articulated by Natividad in a 2022 SparkToro blog post and subsequently developed into a consulting practice, podcast, newsletter, and forthcoming book. The foundational observation is structural: more than half of Google searches now end without a click, and social media platforms — including LinkedIn — algorithmically suppress content that contains outbound links, because every platform's core economic incentive is to keep users inside its own walled garden rather than sending them elsewhere. Natividad's framework does not treat this as a problem to fight; it treats it as the operating reality marketers must build around. The core directive is to "beat the platforms at their own game" by creating content that delivers complete, standalone value natively inside the post itself, with a click being a bonus rather than a requirement.

The framework rests on three named principles. The first, "Platformless Value," instructs marketers to give away their best ideas directly in the post rather than teasing them to force a click — the philosophy being that brand trust compounds when an audience receives real value with no strings attached, much like compounding interest. The second, "Identity-Led Discoverability," argues that a consistent, opinionated point of view is a brand's defensible moat — content that is "distinctly and defensibly" the creator's own is what makes a brand impossible to ignore in an attention-saturated feed, and this cannot be replicated by a competitor's paid campaign. The third, "The Long Game," reframes content strategy as compounding rather than campaign-based: brands that show up consistently, generously, and memorably over time outperform those that only post around launches or promotional moments.

A significant portion of Natividad's broader public work (reflected across her podcast, newsletter, and speaking engagements at venues including Columbia Business School, Cornell, MozCon, and Content Marketing World) addresses the practical measurement problem that Zero Click Marketing creates: if the traditional metric of success — click-through to a website — is deliberately being suppressed by design, what should replace it? Natividad's answer, developed across multiple public talks and interviews, is to reverse-engineer content strategy around qualitative engagement signals: DMs, shares, saves, and unprompted real-world conversations the content generates, rather than only optimizing for last-click attribution. This requires internal alignment — getting a marketing team and its leadership to agree on what "good" content means when the dashboard no longer shows a simple traffic number, which Natividad frames as a cultural and organizational challenge as much as a tactical one.

Natividad's consulting practice, as described on the Zero Click Marketing site, applies this framework specifically to B2B teams across five service areas: content strategy rooted in Zero Click thinking, brand positioning and narrative development, founder-led marketing (explicitly framed as helping founders build a personal brand strategy that "scales company-wide"), fractional marketing director-level advisory for early-stage startups, and team workshops to align organizations around Zero Click frameworks and audience-first thinking. This positions Zero Click Marketing not just as a content tactic but as an integrated go-to-market philosophy specifically aimed at B2B and SaaS-stage companies, with founder voice treated as a primary distribution and trust-building asset.

# Key Ideas

* Platforms (including LinkedIn) are structurally incentivized to suppress content containing outbound links because every platform wants to keep users inside its own ecosystem — this is a permanent design feature, not a temporary algorithm quirk to wait out
* "Zero Click Content" delivers complete, standalone value natively inside the post — the click becomes optional rather than the goal, which paradoxically builds more trust and downstream engagement than content that withholds value to force a click
* A consistent, opinionated point of view ("Identity-Led Discoverability") functions as a brand's competitive moat — content that is distinctly and defensibly the creator's own cannot be replicated by a competitor's paid media spend
* Zero Click Marketing compounds like equity rather than functioning like a campaign — consistency and generosity over time outperform launch-driven or promotional-moment-only content
* Traditional click-through measurement is structurally unsuited to this strategy; marketing teams need qualitative KPIs (DMs, shares, saves, unprompted conversations) and internal alignment on what "good" content means in their absence

# Why It Matters for B2B SaaS

Zero Click Marketing directly resolves one of the central tensions B2B SaaS marketing teams face on LinkedIn: the platform's algorithm penalizes outbound links (as confirmed independently by Richard van der Blom's 2025/2026 research showing median reach drops of 18-19% for single-link posts), yet SaaS companies still need to drive demo requests, content downloads, and pipeline. Natividad's framework reframes the objective — rather than treating every LinkedIn post as a traffic-driving vehicle, SaaS marketers should treat the post itself as the complete deliverable (a genuinely useful framework, data point, or insight), trusting that the resulting brand recognition and trust compound into pipeline through other channels (direct search, inbound demo requests, sales conversations where the prospect already recognizes the brand) rather than requiring an immediate click-through.

For founder-led content and personal branding specifically, Natividad's explicit framing of "founder voice" as a company's most powerful and scalable brand asset gives B2B SaaS marketing leaders a clear mandate: invest in developing the founder's point of view and voice as a strategic asset, not a side project. For demand generation teams measuring LinkedIn's contribution to pipeline, the framework's emphasis on qualitative KPIs (saves, shares, DMs, and direct mentions in sales conversations) provides a more honest and accurate measurement model than forcing LinkedIn performance into last-click attribution reports — which, per van der Blom's research, is exactly the kind of content (link-heavy, click-optimized) that the platform's algorithm now actively suppresses.
