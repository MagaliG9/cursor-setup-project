# Summary

Pierre Herubel, founder of the B2B content agency ContentPath and writer of the newsletter "Pierre's Content Guides" (audience described as having grown to 120,000+ followers and $0 to $1M in business via content), lays out a structured framework for what he calls "team-led content": a strategy where a company's LinkedIn visibility is driven by the personal brands of multiple team members rather than (or in addition to) its company page. The article opens by naming two failure patterns he observes repeatedly when auditing B2B marketing strategies: companies pouring effort into a company page that gets only 200 impressions per post and concluding LinkedIn "doesn't work," and companies that find success with a single profile (often the founder's) but never expand distribution beyond that one voice and network. Herubel cites that company page posts typically reach only 2-5% of followers, while a personal profile post from someone with a comparable connection count can reach 10-30% — a five-to-six-times difference in baseline distribution.

The strategic core of the framework rests on three named psychological effects the article argues team-led content produces for a B2B brand: the "They Are Everywhere" effect (when prospects see content from the CEO, Head of Product, and Growth Lead across different days, the brand begins to feel omnipresent without requiring high volume from any single person); the "They Really Help Us" effect (when content covers genuine audience pain points, the team becomes associated with real day-to-day value rather than promotional noise); and the "They Are Pros" halo effect (when multiple team members post credibly and consistently, each person's credibility reinforces the others', and a prospect's trust extends from individuals to the entire organization). The operational model that follows is explicit: start with 3-5 people (not the whole company), assign each a clear topical "lane" based on a 1:1 interview into their actual expertise and strengths, have each person publish independently from their own profile (not the company page), and use the company page only as a secondary channel for news, jobs, and repurposing top-performing personal content.

The article provides granular tactical guidance on team selection and structure. Selection criteria include subject matter expertise, genuine willingness to be visible (Herubel explicitly warns against forcing reluctant team members to post), and client-facing roles (sales, customer success, product, leadership) that put someone in regular contact with the ICP's actual problems. The recommended "starting lineup" for most B2B companies is a Founder/CEO (vision, market commentary, company-building stories), a Subject Matter Expert (deep technical content — VP Product, Head of Engineering), and a Revenue Leader (customer stories, buying patterns — Head of Sales or Growth). Herubel introduces a three-tier content model mapped to organizational level: Strategic content (high-level insights, vision, industry trends) for C-levels speaking to other executives and investors; Tactical content (frameworks, playbooks, decision frameworks) for managers and senior ICs; and Operational content (tutorials, templates, checklists) for the people doing day-to-day work. The framework explicitly recommends mapping these three content levels to the corresponding seniority level within the company — C-levels post strategic content, managers post tactical content, individual contributors post operational content — so that the team's combined output covers a buyer's entire organization coherently rather than one person attempting to cover all three levels inconsistently.

On execution mechanics, the article recommends a shared content calendar (Notion or Google Sheets), a 15-minute weekly content sync, centralized production support (an internal team or agency handling design/editing/scheduling so subject-matter contributors only need to supply ideas and expertise), and a posting cadence of roughly 3 posts per week per person, staggered across the week so the brand maintains consistent presence without everyone posting simultaneously. On content format, the article recommends a roughly 40% educational infographics / 40% carousels / 20% personal branding posts split, with infographics solving one specific problem each (optimized for saves and shares), carousels used for bigger frameworks and the swipe mechanic's dwell-time benefit, and personal branding posts providing the "human layer" that builds the "like" component of trust (alongside the "know" and "trust" built by educational content). A bonus chapter covers content collaborations with other creators outside the company as an extension of the same team-led logic — co-creating content with non-competing creators whose audience overlaps with the company's ICP, to access "borrowed credibility" and tap two engaged networks simultaneously.

# Key Ideas

* Company pages structurally underperform personal profiles by roughly 5-6x in reach (2-5% vs. 10-30% of followers), making personal-profile-led distribution the foundation of any serious B2B LinkedIn strategy
* Team-led content produces three specific psychological effects on prospects: brand omnipresence ("They Are Everywhere"), perceived real-world value ("They Really Help Us"), and a credibility halo effect across the whole organization ("They Are Pros")
* Each team member needs a distinct, narrow topical "lane" based on their actual expertise (assigned via 1:1 interview) — assigning content without strategic direction produces random, overlapping, unfocused output
* Content should be deliberately mapped to organizational seniority: C-levels post strategic/vision content, managers post tactical frameworks, individual contributors post operational tutorials — together covering every level of a buyer's organization
* Centralized production support (internal team or agency handling design, editing, and scheduling) is what makes multi-profile publishing sustainable; subject-matter contributors should only need to supply ideas and expertise, not execute production themselves

# Why It Matters for B2B SaaS

This framework provides the most operationally complete blueprint in this collection for converting "founder-led content" into a scalable, organization-wide program rather than a single-person initiative — directly relevant to B2B SaaS companies trying to move beyond founder-only content without losing strategic coherence. The three-tier content model (strategic/tactical/operational mapped to seniority) gives SaaS marketing leaders a concrete way to brief a CEO, a VP of Product, and an AE differently, ensuring the company's combined LinkedIn presence speaks to every layer of a target account's buying committee — economic buyer, technical evaluator, and end user — without requiring any single person to artificially stretch across all three registers.

For employee advocacy and community building specifically, the requirement for genuine willingness (not mandated posting), a 1:1-interview-derived topical lane, and centralized production support gives SaaS marketing teams a realistic operating model that avoids the most common failure mode of employee advocacy programs: leadership mandating company-wide posting without strategy, support, or genuine buy-in, which produces low-quality, inconsistent content that damages rather than builds the brand. The content collaboration chapter is also directly applicable to demand generation: B2B SaaS marketers can use creator collaborations with non-competing, audience-adjacent voices (e.g., a marketing-tech SaaS partnering with a sales-tech creator) as a lower-cost alternative to paid co-marketing campaigns, borrowing credibility and tapping two engaged, relevant networks simultaneously.
